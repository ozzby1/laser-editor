Laser photo engraving guide that actually helps

Good photo engraving is not about one magic DPI value or a copied speed setting. It is about choosing the right photo, preparing the tones for the material, testing a small crop, and then making careful changes. This guide walks you through the whole process in plain language: photo selection, contrast, grayscale, dithering, line interval, material behavior, LightBurn workflow, export settings and the small mistakes that often ruin otherwise good engravings.

Quick start: a simple method that saves material

When you are starting out, it is tempting to search for “the best settings” and send the whole photo straight to the laser. That is usually the expensive way to learn. A small test crop tells you much more than a perfect-looking preview on a screen.

Use the same piece of material you will use for the final job, prepare one clean version of the photo, and test only a small area first. Once that crop looks good, the full engraving becomes much more predictable.

Start with the materialAsk one simple question: will the laser make this surface darker, lighter, frosted, bleached, melted, chipped or simply remove a coating? That answer decides whether you should invert the photo and how hard you can push the power.
Pick a photo with real detailSharp eyes, clear edges and a subject that stands apart from the background matter more than a dramatic filter. A slightly plain but clean photo often engraves better than a moody photo with blocked shadows.
Set the final size earlyChoose the real width and height in millimeters before you worry about DPI. If the face is too small on the final object, more DPI will not magically bring back detail.
Prepare one clean imageConvert to grayscale, adjust levels or curves, protect facial detail, sharpen gently after resizing, and invert only when the material needs it. Avoid processing the same image again and again in different programs.
Test a useful cropA good crop is about 25–40 mm wide and includes an eye, hair, skin, a dark area and a bit of background. That tiny sample tells you almost everything you need before engraving the full piece.
A workshop rule worth keeping: change one thing at a time. If you change contrast, DPI, power, speed and dithering all together, you will never know which change helped and which one made the result worse.

How laser photo engraving really works

A laser engraving is not a printed photograph. A monitor creates light; a laser changes a physical surface. On wood it burns. On slate it often makes a pale mark. On glass it frosts or chips. On anodized aluminum it removes or changes a coating. Because of that, the same file can look beautiful on one material and completely wrong on another.

The goal is not to make the darkest possible engraving. The goal is separation. You want the face to be readable, the highlights to stay open, the shadows to keep some texture, and the background to stay in the background.

Tone response

Some materials behave almost like an on/off switch: either they mark or they do not. Those materials usually like dithering. Other surfaces can hold smoother gray tones and may work well with grayscale power control.

Dot growth

A laser dot is rarely the exact size of one image pixel. Heat spreads, wood grain absorbs unevenly, leather fibers swell, glass chips and acrylic melts. When dots grow too much, high DPI can actually hide detail.

Viewing distance

A pendant that someone holds in their hand needs different detail than a wall photo seen from two meters away. The best resolution is the one that looks right at the distance where the object will be viewed.

Three common ways to engrave a photo

  • Dithered black-and-white raster: the photo is converted into small black and white dots. This is predictable and often the safest choice for wood, leather and many coated materials.
  • Grayscale raster: the laser varies power based on the tone of each pixel. It can look smooth, but only if the laser and material respond consistently.
  • Halftone or newsprint style: visible dot patterns are used as a deliberate visual style. It can look great, but it is not always the most natural choice for portraits.
Do not judge only by the preview. A screen cannot show smoke staining, grain, melting, dot overlap, focus errors or mechanical banding. A real test on the real material is the only preview that truly counts.

Laser types and what they change

The laser source matters because different wavelengths behave differently on different materials. A blue diode, CO2 laser, fiber galvo, infrared module and UV laser can all engrave photos, but they do not like the same surfaces and they do not use the same logic.

Practical habit: treat every new combination as a new recipe: laser type, lens, focus, material, finish, image mode and cleaning method. Birch plywood, beech and bamboo may all be “wood”, but they can engrave very differently.

Choosing a photo that will engrave well

The photo sets the ceiling for the whole project. Editing can improve contrast and make the tones more suitable for engraving, but it cannot create real facial detail that is not in the original image.

What usually works

Sharp face, clear eyes, simple lighting, visible hair texture, enough distance between subject and background, and no heavy filters. Window light or soft outdoor light is often better than harsh flash.

What usually causes trouble

Tiny web photos, motion blur, faces in deep shadow, blown-out highlights, busy backgrounds, strong beauty filters, heavy JPEG noise and photos where the subject blends into the background.

A quick photo check

  • Zoom in to the eyes. If the eyes are soft, the engraving will feel soft too.
  • Look at the shadows. If black hair, clothing and background all merge together, separate them before engraving.
  • Check the highlights. Pure white skin or shiny spots can disappear completely on the final material.
  • Crop tightly enough. For portraits, the face should be large enough to carry the engraving.
Simple portrait tip: make the eyes and mouth readable first. People forgive a softer background, but they immediately notice a flat face or dead-looking eyes.

Image preparation workflow

A good workflow keeps the image clean. The common mistake is to keep adding contrast, sharpening and filters until the preview looks punchy. On the laser, that often turns into burned shadows, noisy skin and harsh edges.

Crop for the objectDecide the final physical size and composition first. Remove empty space that does not help the subject.
Convert to grayscaleColor does not engrave directly. What matters is how colors become tones. Red lips, blue eyes and brown hair can become surprisingly similar if the conversion is poor.
Fix the tonal rangeUse levels or curves to open the face, keep highlight detail and stop the shadows from becoming one black block.
Clean up distractionsLighten, blur or remove a busy background. Small background details often become noise after dithering.
Resize, then sharpen gentlySharpen after resizing to the final pixel size. Use enough sharpening to bring back detail, but not so much that skin gets halos or a crunchy texture.
Choose dithering or grayscale onceIf you export a dithered PNG, make sure your laser software does not dither it again. Double dithering is one of the fastest ways to lose detail.

Brightness, contrast and gamma

Brightness moves everything lighter or darker. Contrast stretches tones apart. Gamma changes the midtones without moving the pure black and white points as much. For portraits, gamma is often useful because skin and facial detail live mostly in the midtones.

Sharpening

Sharpening should help the laser find edges, not make the photo look like a phone HDR filter. If you see bright outlines around the nose, eyes or hair in the preview, the laser will probably exaggerate them.

DPI, line interval and physical size

DPI sounds technical, but the practical idea is simple: it controls how many image dots fit into one inch of real space. Line interval is the distance between engraved scan lines. They describe the same spacing from two different directions.

line interval in mm = 25.4 ÷ DPI DPI = 25.4 ÷ line interval in mm

For many photo engravings, 300–333 DPI is a sensible starting point. It is not a law. Soft wood may look better lower because dots spread. Acrylic, anodized aluminum or coated metal may hold finer dots and justify higher DPI. The right value depends on your real laser spot, lens, material and viewing distance.

DPIApprox. line intervalWhen it can make senseRisk

400–5000.064–0.051 mmSharper work on stable surfaces, smaller objects and materials that can hold fine marks.More lines means more heat and more chance of overlap, banding or dark shadows.
600+0.042 mm or lessSpecial cases such as fine anodized aluminum, acrylic or very small high-detail work.Often slower and not always visibly better. On the wrong material it can become muddy.

Pixel size matters too

A 100 mm wide engraving at 300 DPI needs about 1181 pixels across. At 500 DPI it needs about 1969 pixels. If your original face is only a few hundred pixels wide, raising the DPI only creates more pixels mathematically. It does not bring back real detail.

Good starting point: choose the physical size first, then pick a DPI the material can actually hold. Do not use high DPI just because the number looks more professional.

Test and calibration workflow

Calibration is not busywork. It is how you learn what your machine and material are willing to give you. A small test can prevent a full-size portrait from becoming too dark, too pale or reversed.

Make a useful test crop

Use a crop that includes the hard parts of the photo: one eye, skin, hair, dark clothing and a bit of background. A crop of only the forehead or only the background will not tell you enough.

Test in a controlled grid

Keep the image the same and vary only one or two settings at a time. A practical first grid might compare two speeds, three power levels and two line intervals. Once you find a promising cell, make a second smaller grid around that result.

Power

Too little power gives a pale, weak image. Too much power closes shadows, burns wood, melts acrylic or chips glass too aggressively.

Speed

Slower speed usually adds energy. Faster speed can keep detail cleaner, but only if the mark is still strong enough.

Interval / DPI

Tighter spacing gives more lines, but also more heat and more overlap. Wider spacing can look cleaner on materials with dot bleed.

Write settings down. A notebook or sample board with material, lens, focus, power, speed, interval, image mode and cleaning method will save you hours later.

Dithering algorithms in plain language

Dithering turns gray tones into a pattern of black and white dots. From a normal viewing distance, your eye blends those dots into lighter and darker areas. It is especially useful when the material cannot reliably hold smooth grayscale.

Dithering methodHow it tends to lookWhere it is usefulWhat to watch
Floyd-SteinbergBalanced, familiar and detailed. A good first choice for many photos.General portrait work, wood, leather, slate, coated materials.Can make noisy backgrounds or skin grain more visible if the photo is already rough.
AtkinsonLighter, softer and slightly vintage-looking.Photos that engrave too dark, soft portraits, materials where heavy shadows close quickly.May lose some deep shadow detail compared with stronger algorithms.
JarvisSmoother tonal transitions with a more distributed pattern.Skin tones and large soft gradients when the material can hold fine detail.Can become too dense or slow on materials with strong dot growth.
StuckiDetailed and slightly smoother than Floyd in many images.Portraits where you want detail but less harshness than a very crisp pattern.Needs testing; on some surfaces it can still darken midtones.
Ordered / BayerRegular, visible pattern. More graphic than photographic.Logos, illustrations, stylized images, some glass or acrylic workflows.The pattern can look artificial on faces if used without intention.

Should you dither in the editor or in laser software?

Either can work. The important part is consistency. If you prepare a dithered black-and-white PNG, preserve that pattern in the laser software. If you let LightBurn, LaserGRBL or XCS do the dithering, send it a clean grayscale image and choose the algorithm there.

Avoid double dithering. A dithered image already contains its dot pattern. If another program dithers it again, fine detail can turn into noise and the face can lose shape.

Material-specific advice

Material choice changes everything. The same portrait that needs more contrast on wood may need inversion on slate, softer power on glass and a completely different DPI on anodized aluminum.

Starting settings and test ideas

There is no universal setting table that works for every laser. A 10 W diode, 60 W CO2 and 30 W fiber galvo are completely different tools. Even two pieces of the same wood can react differently. Use these notes as a starting direction, not as final values.

Wood and bamboo

Start with moderate DPI, medium speed and lower power than you expect. If the face gets muddy, reduce energy, widen the line interval or lighten the shadows in the image.

Slate and dark materials

Test inversion early. You are often creating a light image on a dark surface, so the normal wood logic does not apply.

Acrylic, glass and metal

Use small tests and watch heat. These materials can look great, but they often punish too much power or the wrong dot spacing.

A useful first test matrix

  • Choose one prepared crop and keep it unchanged.
  • Test three power levels around a safe middle value.
  • Test two speeds or two line intervals, not ten things at once.
  • Write the result directly on the back or next to the sample.
  • Repeat with a smaller range once you see the best direction.

If a test looks almost right but slightly dark, do not immediately redesign the whole image. Try a little less power, a little more speed or a slightly wider interval first. If the shape of the face is wrong, then return to the image preparation.

Software workflow

Laser software is powerful, but it can also change your image in ways you did not intend. Before engraving, check whether the software is scaling the file, inverting it, dithering it again or applying a different image mode.

LightBurn

Check image mode, line interval, negative image, pass-through, overscan and whether you are using grayscale or a dithering mode. LightBurn gives you a lot of control, so it is worth saving known-good material presets.

LaserGRBL

Pay attention to dithering, line-to-line options, size and speed. It is easy to get a usable result, but the source image must be prepared cleanly.

xTool XCS and similar apps

Check bitmap mode, algorithm, DPI or quality settings, and laser source selection where available. Manufacturer presets are helpful, but photos still need small material tests.

Pass-through and pre-dithered files

If you already created a final black-and-white dithered PNG, the laser software should preserve those pixels as much as possible. In software that supports pass-through or a true black-and-white workflow, use it to avoid changing the dot pattern.

Overscan and borders

Raster engraving needs room for the head to accelerate and decelerate. If the machine allows overscan, use it correctly so the edges of the photo do not become darker or lighter than the center.

Export and file handling

For photo engraving, keep the final file simple and predictable. PNG is usually a safe choice because it preserves pixels without adding JPEG compression artifacts. BMP can also be useful in some laser workflows. SVG is excellent for vectors, but it is usually not the right final format for a dithered photo.

FormatUse it whenAvoid it when
PNGYou want a clean raster file for grayscale or dithered photo engraving.You need a vector cutting file rather than a photo raster.
BMPYour laser software prefers simple uncompressed raster files.File size is a problem or your workflow handles PNG better.
JPGYou only have it as the original source and it is high quality.You are exporting the final dithered file. JPEG compression can damage fine dot patterns.
SVGYou are working with vector shapes, logos, outlines or cut paths.You are trying to preserve final photo pixels or a dithered pattern.

Check scaling on import

After importing the file into laser software, confirm the physical width and height. A perfect image can fail if the software imports it at the wrong size or resamples it unexpectedly.

Keep your source file

Save the edited grayscale version as well as the final exported version. If the test shows that only the contrast or gamma needs a small change, you do not want to start from scratch.

Safety: do not skip this part

Photo engraving often means long raster jobs, smoke, fumes and repeated tests. That makes ventilation, fire safety and material choice especially important.

  • Never engrave unknown plastics. PVC and some other materials can release dangerous gases and damage the machine.
  • Use proper ventilation and filtration for the material you are engraving.
  • Do not leave the laser unattended during a job.
  • Keep the lens, mirrors, bed and air assist clean.
  • Use eye protection and enclosure rules appropriate for your laser type.
  • Follow the manual for your exact machine, not only settings found online.
Important: if you do not know what a material is, do not engrave it. A nice photo is not worth toxic fumes, fire risk or damaged optics.

Advanced techniques once the basics work

After you can make a reliable test crop, you can start improving the final look with more controlled edits. These techniques are not magic fixes, but they can turn a good engraving into a much better one.

Make separate versions for different materials

A wood version may need open shadows and gentle sharpening. A slate version may need inversion and stronger midtone contrast. A dark anodized aluminum version may need finer detail and a different dot density.

Edit locally, not only globally

One brightness slider affects the whole photo. Real portraits usually need more care: brighten the eyes, protect skin highlights, separate hair from the background and reduce detail where it distracts.

Simplify the background

For portraits, the background should support the person, not fight for attention. Lightening, blurring or removing it often improves the engraving more than adding more sharpness.

Try intentional defocus carefully

A very small defocus can soften harsh dots on some materials, especially glass or acrylic. It can also destroy fine detail, so test it and write down the offset.

Watch scan direction

Wood grain, brushed metal and fabric texture can interact with the scan direction. If the grain dominates the portrait, test rotating the image or changing the scan angle if your machine supports it safely.

Use multiple passes only when there is a reason

For photos, one controlled pass is often cleaner than several hot passes. Multiple passes can help some special materials, but they can also darken shadows, create registration issues and add heat damage.

Finish the material properly

Cleaning and finishing can change contrast. Wood may need brushing and sealing. Slate can look different after washing. Acrylic may need paint fill, backing or lighting to show the photo clearly.

Final checklist before the full engraving

Use this list after the test crop looks good and before you commit to the full-size job.

Image

Eyes readable, subject separated from background, shadows not crushed, highlights not empty, no visible JPEG damage and no harsh sharpening halos.

Size

Final millimeter size confirmed, face large enough, pixel count suitable for the chosen DPI and no accidental scaling after import.

Laser setup

Focus, lens, air assist, scan direction, overscan, bed position and material hold-down match the successful test crop.

Processing

Correct inversion, one dithering method only, pass-through used when needed and material-specific settings recorded.

Best long-term habit: keep a small sample library. A few labeled test pieces from your own laser and materials will become more useful than any generic settings table online.

FAQ

What DPI is best for laser photo engraving?

There is no single best DPI. Around 300–333 DPI is a good starting point for many photo jobs. Lower values can look cleaner on wood or stone when dots spread. Higher values can help on acrylic, anodized aluminum or other surfaces that hold very fine marks. The right DPI is the one your laser spot and material can actually reproduce.

Should I use Floyd-Steinberg, Jarvis, Stucki or Atkinson?

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki if you want a safe comparison. Try Atkinson when the result is too dark or you want a softer, lighter feel. Try Jarvis when you want smoother skin tones and the material can hold fine detail. Compare them on the same small crop, not on different photos.

Should photos be inverted?

Invert when the laser mark becomes lighter than the original surface, such as slate, glass, dark acrylic or dark anodized aluminum. Do not invert for light wood when the burned areas become darker.

Is grayscale better than dithering?

Sometimes, but not always. Grayscale can look smooth when the machine and material respond predictably to power changes. Dithering is often safer on materials that behave more like on/off surfaces, because it creates tones with black and white dots instead of relying on perfect power modulation.

Why does my screen preview look better than the engraving?

A monitor is bright, smooth and backlit. A laser creates physical marks on a surface with grain, smoke, melting, chipping or coating removal. That is why a real test crop is more trustworthy than any preview.

Can I engrave any web photo?

Only if it has enough real pixels for the final size and the tones are usable. A 72 DPI web image is not automatically bad if it is large enough in pixels and you scale it down. But enlarging a tiny photo rarely creates real detail.

Why should I avoid double dithering?

Dithering creates a specific black-and-white dot pattern. If another program dithers that pattern again, the image can become noisy, uneven and less detailed. A pre-dithered file should normally be preserved with pass-through or a true black-and-white workflow.

PNG, BMP, JPG or SVG?

For photo engraving, PNG or BMP are usually the safest final raster formats. JPG is fine as a high-quality source, but repeated JPG exports can damage detail. SVG is mainly for vectors, not for preserving final dithered photo pixels.
