Quick start: the reliable five-step method

Laser photo engraving is the process of translating a continuous-tone photograph into physical marks that a laser and a material can reproduce. The best results come from controlling the image before the laser job, then validating the settings on a small test crop.

1. Pick a photo with readable light.Choose clear facial features, visible eyes, separated hair/background, and enough midtones. Avoid backlit faces, heavy filters, compressed screenshots and very dark shadows with no detail.
2. Decide the real engraved size first.Set the final width and height in millimeters or inches before choosing DPI. A 70 mm pendant photo and a 300 mm wall plaque need different pixel counts and different line intervals.
3. Prepare the grayscale image.Crop, remove distractions, convert to grayscale, adjust levels/curves, protect important details, brighten shadows when needed, and sharpen only after resizing.
4. Choose the output method.Use dithering for predictable black/white dot engraving. Use grayscale only when your machine and material can produce stable intermediate tones.
5. Test a crop before the final job.Engrave a 20–50 mm crop containing a face, hair, background and shadow. Compare power, speed, DPI and dithering modes. Save the winning settings.
The most important rule: do not chase quality by blindly increasing DPI. Once the lines overlap more than the material can handle, the result becomes slower, darker and muddier instead of sharper.

Core principles: what the laser is really doing

A screen emits light. A laser engraving reflects light from a physical surface. That difference explains why a beautiful monitor preview can still engrave badly. The laser does not print ink; it changes the material by burning, bleaching, foaming, melting, frosting, ablating a coating, oxidizing a surface or bonding a marking compound.

Pixel

The image unit. Pixel count determines whether a source image has enough real detail for the chosen physical size.

Dot / mark

The physical laser mark on the material. It can be larger than the software pixel because of beam diameter, heat spread, smoke staining and material grain.

Line interval

The distance between raster scan lines. DPI is simply 25.4 divided by line interval in millimeters.

Tonal range

The number of visually distinct light-to-dark levels the material can show. Some surfaces have many levels; others are mostly on/off.

Dot gain

The physical mark becomes wider or darker than expected. Wood, leather and paper often have strong dot gain.

Material contrast

The difference between untouched surface and engraved mark. Low-contrast materials require stronger image preparation and careful finishing.

Great photo engraving is therefore a balance of four things: image tone, dot pattern, physical line spacing and material reaction. Changing one without the others often creates new problems.

Choosing a photo that will engrave well

The photo itself sets the ceiling for quality. A poor source can be improved, but it cannot become a perfect engraving simply by changing the laser mode.

Good candidates

Clear subject, visible eyes, natural contrast, directional light, separated face and hair, simple background, enough pixels, low noise, no heavy blur.

Difficult candidates

Backlit faces, dark hair on a dark background, white clothing on a white background, tiny group faces, strong beauty filters, JPEG blocks, screenshots.

Portrait checklist

  • Eyes: catchlights and eyelids must remain visible after grayscale conversion.
  • Skin: avoid harsh contrast that turns skin into spots, but keep enough midtone separation to avoid a flat face.
  • Hair: dark hair needs lifted shadows; light hair needs local contrast so it does not disappear.
  • Background: simplify or remove distracting backgrounds. A busy background steals dot detail from the face.
  • Crop: fill the engraving with the important subject. Tiny faces are hard to engrave sharply.

Minimum useful resolution

Calculate required pixels from final size, not from the image’s metadata label. For example, a 100 mm wide engraving at 318 DPI needs roughly 1252 pixels across: 100 ÷ 25.4 × 318. More source pixels are helpful for editing, but the final exported raster only needs enough pixels for the chosen size and line interval.

Image preparation workflow in detail

Most failed photo engravings are image-preparation failures. Prepare the image for the material, not for the screen.

DPI, line interval and physical size

DPI in laser engraving is not magic “quality”. It is line density. Higher DPI means more scan lines per inch, smaller line interval and usually longer run time. It can improve detail only until the material and laser spot can no longer separate the lines.

DPI / LPILine intervalPractical useRisk
150 DPI0.169 mmLarge viewing distance, rough wood, fast tests, rustic signs.Visible scan lines on close-up photos.
200 DPI0.127 mmSoft wood, leather, large photos, materials with strong dot gain.Lower fine detail, but often cleaner than too much DPI.
254 DPI0.100 mmGood metric-friendly starting point for many diode/CO2 jobs.May still be too dense for very smoky wood.
318 DPI0.080 mmCommon practical photo range for balanced detail and speed.Can darken wood if power is not reduced.
333 DPI0.076 mmPopular compromise for CO2/diode photo engraving.Not automatically better than 254/300.
500 DPI0.051 mmAcrylic, anodized aluminum, fine detail surfaces, small objects.Slower; can become muddy on absorbent materials.
600 DPI0.042 mmVery fine details where the material holds small marks.Easy to overdo; heat overlap and long run time.
1000 DPI0.025 mmSpecial cases on galvo/fiber or very fine surfaces.Usually unnecessary for wood photos; demands precise optics and motion.

Useful formulas

  • Line interval in mm = 25.4 ÷ DPI
  • Pixels wide = width in inches × DPI
  • Width in inches = width in mm ÷ 25.4
  • Estimated scan lines = engraving height in inches × DPI

How to choose the best line interval

Run an interval test on the real material using the lens and focus you will use for the job. If the lines overlap and darken, lower DPI or reduce power. If you see gaps, increase DPI or use a slightly larger dot through focus/power adjustment. The ideal line interval is tied to the real mark width, not only to the theoretical laser spot.

Do not trust image metadata alone. Many PNG/JPG files carry metadata that software interprets differently. The reliable method is to set final physical size and line interval inside the laser workflow, or use pass-through carefully when the image has already been resized and dithered.

Dithering algorithms: which one to choose

Dithering converts a grayscale photo into a black-and-white dot pattern. Dark areas receive many black dots; light areas receive fewer dots. Different algorithms distribute those dots differently, which changes texture, contrast and perceived detail.

AlgorithmLookBest useWatch out for
Floyd-SteinbergSharp, detailed, balanced error diffusion.Best first test for portraits, wood, slate, leather and general photos.Can create grain or diagonal artifacts in smooth areas.
AtkinsonLighter, softer, more open highlights.Faces, vintage style, materials that engrave too dark, diode on wood/leather.Can lose deep shadow density and very fine texture.
JarvisSmooth, tonal, rich dot distribution.Skin tones, animals, landscapes, larger engravings with enough resolution.Can look soft or heavy on small engravings.
StuckiDetailed but slightly smoother than Floyd in many cases.Architecture, fur, hair, textured materials and high-detail photos.May darken if the material has strong dot gain.
Sierra / BurkesOften smoother than Floyd with a different grain structure.When available, useful for comparison on portraits and gradients.Software support varies.
Ordered / BayerRegular visible pattern.Logos, graphics, predictable gradients, some glass/acrylic workflows.Can look artificial on faces and may show a grid texture.
HalftonePrint-like dots or cells.Stylized posters, newspaper look, graphic projects.Less photorealistic and sensitive to cell size/angle.
ThresholdPure black/white cutoff.Logos, silhouettes, line art, text, prepared high-contrast artwork.Poor for normal photos because midtones disappear.

Beginner default

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki, then compare Atkinson if the result is too dark or harsh.

Portrait default

Test Floyd-Steinberg, Atkinson and Jarvis on a face crop. Pick the one with readable eyes and natural skin.

Logo/graphic default

Use Threshold for pure black/white artwork. Use Ordered/Bayer for controlled pattern-style gradients.

Never dither twice. If PhotoEngraving.online exports a dithered PNG/BMP, do not let the laser software dither it again. Use pass-through or a mode that preserves the original pixels.

Grayscale, threshold, halftone and pass-through

Dithering is not the only way to engrave photos. Some machines and materials can use grayscale power modulation, while others need strict black/white data.

When grayscale is worth testing

Use grayscale when a test wedge shows many distinct levels from light to dark without sudden burning, banding or muddy shadows. If only a few levels are visible, dithering usually gives a better photo.

When pass-through matters

Pass-through is important when your file already contains the final dot pattern. Resizing or reprocessing a dithered image can create moiré, blur and broken dots. The physical size, pixel count and line interval must already agree before you use it.

Focus, lens, air assist and machine motion

Image processing cannot compensate for poor focus, dirty optics or unstable motion. Mechanical and optical setup often decide whether fine dots stay sharp.

Focus

Accurate focus creates the smallest, most consistent mark. Slight intentional defocus can soften harsh dots, but it should be tested deliberately, not caused by poor setup.

Lens choice

Shorter focal lenses can create finer detail on flat materials; longer lenses help with larger areas, uneven surfaces and deeper focus tolerance.

Air assist

Strong air can reduce flame and soot, but it can also cool the mark or change tone. Photo engraving sometimes needs lower air than cutting.

Overscan & acceleration

Raster jobs need enough overscan so the head reaches stable speed before engraving. Poor acceleration settings can cause darker edges or banding.

Clean optics

Smoke on lenses or mirrors reduces power and creates inconsistent marks. Long photo jobs create a lot of residue.

Flatness

Warped wood, curved objects and uneven slate shift focus across the image. Use jigs, hold-downs or choose a more forgiving lens.

Material workflows

Each material has a different tonal language. Prepare separate image versions for wood, slate, glass, acrylic and metal instead of using one universal file.

Wood, plywood and bamboo

Wood is popular but difficult because grain, glue, density and smoke all affect contrast. Light, fine-grain woods usually show photos better than dark or strongly figured woods. Basswood, maple, birch plywood and bamboo can work well; oak and highly varied woods can hide facial detail.

  • Use lower-to-medium DPI first, often around 200–333 DPI depending on dot gain.
  • Brighten shadows before dithering so dark hair and eyes do not become solid blocks.
  • Use moderate air assist and clean smoke residue after engraving.
  • Masking can reduce smoke stains but may soften tiny dot detail or add cleanup work.
  • Test finishing oil/varnish on scrap because it can darken the wood and change contrast.

Slate and dark stone

Slate usually engraves lighter than the original surface, so photos often need inversion. It can produce dramatic high-contrast portraits, especially with strong midtone preparation.

  • Invert the image for most dark slate workflows.
  • Use enough contrast for faces, but avoid blowing out large white patches.
  • Clean after engraving; dust changes perceived contrast.
  • Slate varies greatly. Test each batch and even each tile when quality matters.

Glass and mirrors

Glass does not burn like wood; it frosts, chips or micro-fractures. The best result is often a controlled light frost rather than maximum power.

  • Use low-to-moderate power and avoid excessive heat.
  • Ordered dithering or a softer algorithm can work well for smooth glass tones.
  • Wet paper towel, dish soap, transfer tape or masking methods are often tested to reduce chipping.
  • For mirrors, decide whether engraving from the front or back gives the desired look.

Acrylic

Acrylic can engrave beautifully, especially cast acrylic. Clear acrylic usually responds better to CO2 than blue diode. Dark or opaque acrylic may respond differently depending on pigment and wavelength.

  • Invert when the engraved mark becomes lighter than the surface or when edge-lit/back-lit.
  • Use clean focus and avoid excessive heat that melts tiny details.
  • Cast acrylic usually engraves cleaner than extruded acrylic.
  • Consider backside engraving for signs and photo panels.

Ceramic tile and the Norton White Tile method

White ceramic tile can be marked by using coatings or titanium-dioxide-rich paint methods. The laser bonds or changes the coating to create a dark image. This workflow is highly dependent on paint, tile, laser wavelength and cleaning process.

  • Prepare a test grid with the exact paint and tile combination.
  • Use a face/detail crop first, not the full photo.
  • Allow coatings to dry fully and keep application thickness consistent.
  • Use safe ventilation and confirm paint/coating suitability before lasering.

Anodized aluminum and coated metals

Photo engraving on anodized or coated metal usually removes or changes a surface layer. It can hold very fine detail and may support higher DPI than wood.

  • Dark anodized aluminum often needs inversion because the laser creates a light mark.
  • Higher DPI can be useful if the machine and coating can resolve it.
  • Keep the surface clean; fingerprints can affect marks.
  • For tumblers and curved objects, rotary setup and focus consistency are critical.

Stainless steel and bare metals

CO2 and diode lasers usually need a marking compound or coating for durable dark marks on stainless steel. Fiber/IR lasers are much better suited for direct metal marking.

  • Use the correct laser source for the metal.
  • Test marking spray/compound separately from photo dithering.
  • For fiber galvos, dot duration, frequency, hatch and lens field settings can matter as much as image DPI.

Leather, paper, cardboard and fabric

These materials mark easily but burn, curl or discolor quickly. Photos often need lower power, higher speed and open dithering.

  • Use real leather with known tanning process. Avoid unknown artificial leather and PVC/vinyl materials.
  • Atkinson or lower-density dithering can prevent overly dark skin tones.
  • Paper and cardboard ignite easily; never leave the machine unattended.
  • Fabric may need low power, fast speed and test strips because synthetic fibers can melt or release fumes.
Material identity matters. “Acrylic,” “leather,” “plastic” and “coated metal” are broad categories. Always confirm the exact material and coating before building a repeatable preset.

Power, speed and DPI relationship

Higher power, slower speed and higher DPI all increase delivered energy per area. If you increase DPI, you often need to reduce power or increase speed. If you lower DPI, you may need slightly more power per mark to maintain contrast.

The testing method professionals rely on

Testing is not wasted time. It is the fastest path to a repeatable preset. A single full-size failed engraving usually wastes more time and material than several small tests.

1. Crop test

Use a small crop containing eyes, hair, highlights, midtones and shadow. A good crop predicts the final engraving better than a generic test square.

2. Power/speed matrix

Keep image and DPI fixed. Test several powers or speeds. Choose the most detailed version, not automatically the darkest.

3. DPI/interval matrix

Keep power/speed fixed. Compare two or three line intervals. Stop increasing DPI when detail stops improving.

4. Dither matrix

Keep laser settings fixed. Compare Floyd, Atkinson, Jarvis, Stucki and Ordered/Bayer on the same crop.

5. Finishing test

Clean, seal, oil, wash or backlight the test exactly as the final product will be finished.

6. Record settings

Write down material, lens, focus, air, speed, power, DPI, dither mode, software version and finish.

What to record in your preset notes

  • Machine, laser source, wattage, lens, focus method and bed height.
  • Material supplier, thickness, color, coating and batch if known.
  • Software, image mode, DPI/interval, speed, power, passes, air assist and overscan.
  • Photo preparation values: inversion, brightness, contrast, gamma, sharpening and dithering algorithm.
  • Post-processing: cleaning, brushing, washing, sealing, oiling, painting or backlighting.

Software workflow: PhotoEngraving.online, LightBurn, LaserGRBL and XCS

PhotoEngraving.online

Use PhotoEngraving.online as the image-preparation stage: load the photo, choose normal or negative output, set physical size and DPI, tune brightness/contrast/gamma/sharpness, choose dithering and export a laser-ready PNG or BMP.

LightBurn

LightBurn can process images directly with several image modes, but if the PNG/BMP is already resized and dithered, use pass-through or a workflow that preserves the prepared pixels. Confirm that physical size, DPI/line interval and layer mode agree before running the job.

LaserGRBL

LaserGRBL supports raster import, grayscale line-to-line engraving and 1-bit dithering. For materials that do not react linearly to power, dithering is often more predictable than grayscale.

xTool Creative Space / galvo-style workflows

XCS and galvo workflows can use grayscale, dithering, dot duration, power, speed and laser-source choices. On machines with both blue and IR/fiber sources, choose the source according to material absorption, not according to photo appearance alone.

General rule for all software

Only one tool should be responsible for final dithering. If the photo editor has already created the dot pattern, the laser software should not resize or re-dither it.

Advanced techniques

Material-specific image versions

Create separate processed files for each material. A wood version may need lifted shadows and lower DPI. A slate version may need inversion and stronger midtones. A dark anodized aluminum version may need high DPI and crisp sharpening.

Selective editing with masks

Edit faces, hair, clothing and background separately. A single global brightness slider rarely preserves everything. Brighten dark hair while keeping eyes sharp; soften skin while preserving facial features.

Background control

For portraits, removing or fading the background often improves the perceived engraving more than increasing DPI. Background dots compete with facial detail.

Controlled defocus

A tiny intentional defocus can soften harsh dots on glass, acrylic or aggressive diode burns. It can also destroy fine detail, so compare focused and slightly defocused samples.

Multi-pass strategy

For most photos, one clean pass is better than multiple hot passes. Multiple passes can help special coatings or tile methods, but they can also darken shadows and create registration errors.

Two-stage engraving

Some advanced workflows engrave a light photo pass first, then add a second pass only for text, border, silhouette or selected deep shadows. Keep this separate from blindly repeating the full image.

Viewing distance

A photo viewed from one meter does not need the same detail as a pendant viewed from 20 cm. Large wall pieces can use lower DPI and stronger contrast; small keepsakes need cleaner source photos and careful focus.

Finishing for contrast

Cleaning, brushing, washing, oiling, sealing or backlighting can change the final appearance as much as a laser parameter. Always finish the test sample the same way as the final product.

Safety: do not skip this

Photo engravings can be long raster jobs, which means extended smoke, heat and fire risk. Use a proper enclosure, exhaust, suitable filtration where needed, correct eye protection for your laser wavelength and continuous supervision.

Never process unknown plastics or coatings. PVC/vinyl and chlorinated materials can release dangerous corrosive fumes. Some artificial leathers, coated fabrics, resins and plastics are unsafe or unknown. If you cannot verify the material, do not laser it.
  • Never leave a running laser unattended.
  • Keep a suitable fire extinguisher nearby and know how to use it.
  • Use ventilation that removes fumes from the workspace, not just an open window.
  • Clean soot, scraps and dust from the machine before long raster jobs.
  • Do not engrave reflective bare metal with an unsuitable open laser setup.
  • Check the material Safety Data Sheet when using plastics, coatings, paints, leather or unknown composites.
  • Follow the machine manufacturer’s instructions for enclosure, interlocks, eyewear and exhaust.

Final checklist before running a full photo engraving

Image

Correct crop, enough pixels, grayscale prepared, important details visible, final size decided, sharpened after resizing.

Output

Correct DPI/line interval, correct inversion, chosen dither/grayscale mode, lossless export, no double dithering.

Machine

Clean optics, correct focus, flat material, appropriate air assist, overscan set, rotary/jig aligned if needed.

Material

Known safe material, tested batch, cleaned surface, masking/coating consistent, finish tested.

Safety

Enclosure closed, ventilation on, fire watch active, extinguisher nearby, no unknown plastics or unsafe coatings.

Small crop test approved under final lighting and final post-processing method.

FAQ

What DPI is best for laser photo engraving?

There is no universal best DPI. Around 254–333 DPI is a practical starting range for many photo jobs, lower can be better on wood or leather with dot gain, and 500–600 DPI can be useful on acrylic or anodized surfaces that hold fine marks. Confirm with an interval test.

Should I use dithering or grayscale?

Use dithering when the material behaves mostly on/off or when you want predictable shading through dot density. Use grayscale only when your laser and material can produce stable intermediate tones or controlled depth.

Which dithering algorithm should I start with?

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki. Try Atkinson for lighter, softer portraits or materials that engrave too dark. Try Jarvis for smoother tones on larger photos. Ordered/Bayer is useful for logos, graphics and some glass/acrylic looks.

Should I invert the photo?

Invert when the laser creates a light mark on a dark material, such as slate, glass, dark acrylic or dark anodized aluminum. Do not invert for light wood when the laser burn becomes darker than the surface.

Why is my engraving darker than the preview?

The material has dot gain or you are delivering too much energy per area. Reduce power, increase speed, lower DPI, brighten midtones or use a lighter dithering algorithm such as Atkinson.

Can I use JPG files?

A high-quality JPG can be used as a source, but repeated JPG saving creates compression artifacts. For the final laser-ready image, use PNG or BMP when possible.

Is higher laser power better for photos?

No. Photo engraving is usually about controlled contrast, not maximum depth. Too much power burns shadows together, increases smoke and removes fine detail.

Can I engrave any material?

No. Some materials are unsafe or unsuitable, especially PVC/vinyl, unknown plastics and some coated materials. Always verify material composition and follow safety guidance.
