Quick start: the reliable five-step method

Photo engraving is not just “send a JPG to the laser”. The best results come from controlling the image first, then testing the material. Use this fast workflow whenever you start a new photo, machine, lens or material batch.

Choose a photo with real separation.Good light, visible eyes, clean subject edges and a balanced mix of highlights, midtones and shadows are more important than extreme resolution.
Crop to the final physical size.Decide the engraved width and height in millimeters before choosing DPI. A 100 mm portrait and a 250 mm portrait do not need the same pixel dimensions.
Prepare grayscale tones.Remove distractions, convert to grayscale, adjust levels/curves, protect facial detail, add moderate sharpening and avoid crushed black areas.
Choose the output method.Use dithering for predictable black/white dot engraving. Use grayscale only when the laser and material can reproduce real intermediate tones.
Run a small test matrix.Engrave a 20–40 mm crop with several power/speed or interval variants. Keep notes. The final large engraving should only run after the small crop looks right.
Rule of thumb: if a photo looks too dark after engraving, do not immediately increase DPI. First reduce power, increase speed, lower contrast in the shadows, or choose a dithering mode that opens the midtones.

How laser photo engraving works

A laser can only do a limited set of things at each tiny point: mark, burn, ablate, melt, frost, bleach, remove coating, or change color. A photograph, however, contains continuous tones. The whole art of photo engraving is converting those tones into marks your exact laser and material can reproduce.

Raster engraving

The laser scans the image line by line. The line spacing is the line interval, and its inverse is DPI/LPI. Too tight can make burns overlap; too loose can leave visible gaps.

Dithering

The image is converted into black and white dots. More dots create darker areas; fewer dots create lighter areas. This is usually the safest photo method.

Grayscale power modulation

The laser varies power or dot duration for each pixel. It can be beautiful on the right material, but it is less forgiving and depends on linear material response.

Many materials are not linear. A little power may do nothing, then a tiny increase suddenly burns dark. For those materials, dithering is easier because every engraved dot uses a chosen mark power while tonal value is controlled by dot density.

The same image can behave very differently on a blue diode, CO2 tube, fiber galvo, infrared diode or UV laser. Material absorption and spot size matter more than the brand name.

Choosing a photo that will engrave well

The source photo decides half of the result. A technically sharp but badly lit photo can engrave worse than a smaller image with clear tonal separation.

Good candidates

Clear subject, visible eyes, simple background, soft but directional light, no heavy phone noise, no crushed shadows, enough contrast between face/hair/clothes/background.

Difficult candidates

Backlit faces, black hair on black background, white clothing on white background, tiny faces in group photos, strong filters, low-resolution screenshots, JPEG artifacts.

Portrait tips

  • Eyes must survive. If the eyes disappear in the processed preview, the engraving will feel dead.
  • Open the shadows. Dark hair and dark clothes often merge into one burnt mass unless you lighten them before dithering.
  • Simplify backgrounds. A busy forest, room or crowd competes with the face. Blur, remove or lighten it.
  • Use local contrast, not just global contrast. Increasing contrast globally can destroy highlights and shadows. Curves or selective masks are better.

DPI, line interval and physical size

DPI is often misunderstood. On a laser it is not a magic quality slider. It is a way of describing how close the raster lines or pixels are. If your laser spot and material burn are larger than the pixel spacing, extra DPI can make the engraving darker, slower and muddier.

How to find your real best DPI

  1. Engrave a small line interval test on the real material.
  2. Look under good light or magnification. The ideal interval makes adjacent lines just touch without becoming a solid smear.
  3. Use that interval as your maximum useful detail. More density usually increases time and darkness rather than real detail.
  4. Repeat after changing lens, focus, speed, power, air assist or material finish.

Dithering algorithms: which one to choose

Dithering converts grayscale into black and white dots. Different algorithms create different dot textures. There is no single winner; choose by image type, material, viewing distance and taste.

Beginner choice

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki at a conservative DPI. Adjust brightness/gamma before changing everything else.

Portrait choice

Test Floyd-Steinberg, Atkinson and Jarvis on a small face crop. Pick the one that keeps eyes and skin tones readable.

Logo/graphic choice

Use Threshold for pure black/white artwork, or Ordered/Bayer for predictable gradients and clean repeated textures.

Material-specific advice

Every material has its own “tone language”. Some get darker when engraved. Some get lighter. Some melt, frost, bleach or expose an underlayer. The same photo should often be inverted, sharpened or softened differently for each surface.

Wood, plywood and bamboo

Usually not invertedDithering recommendedLower to medium DPI

  • Light, fine-grain wood is easiest. Birch, maple, basswood and bamboo can work well; strong grain can compete with facial detail.
  • Use moderate power. Too much power makes all dark areas merge and removes detail.
  • Sand consistently before engraving. Uneven surface height changes focus and tone.
  • Air assist can reduce smoke staining, but too much air may reduce darkness or produce uneven tone on some setups.
  • After engraving, gently brush or clean soot. Do not judge final contrast while the surface is still covered with residue.

Slate and dark stone

Invert imageEngraved areas become lighterTest power carefully

  • Slate often gives a bright gray/white mark, so use a negative image workflow.
  • Keep the process shallow. Too much power can chip or flatten delicate tones.
  • Clean, dry and inspect the surface first. Some slate has veins or uneven coatings.
  • For photos, a small power matrix is more useful than guessing from someone else’s exact settings.

Acrylic

Often inverted on dark/clear acrylicCO2 excels on clear acrylicHigher DPI often possible

  • Cast acrylic generally engraves cleaner than extruded acrylic for frosted photo effects.
  • Blue diode lasers usually need opaque/darker acrylic; clear and blue acrylic are difficult because of wavelength absorption.
  • Opaque acrylic can hold smoother, cleaner photo detail than wood because it has no grain.
  • Remove protective film for engraving unless your test proves it helps. Film can melt into fine photo details.

Glass and mirrors

Often invertedUse masking or moist paperAvoid heat overload

  • Glass engraves by micro-fracturing/frosting the surface, not by creating a black burn.
  • A single moist paper towel layer can reduce roughness and produce a clearer white mark. Avoid bubbles and overlaps.
  • Lower heat is often better. Too much power creates chips, craters and rough patches.
  • Use a rotary attachment for glasses and mugs. Keep handles out of the laser path.

Anodized aluminum and coated metals

Usually inverted on dark anodized surfacesHigh detail possibleFiber/IR or CO2 depending on coating

  • Dark anodized aluminum can hold very fine bright marks, so higher DPI may be useful.
  • Coated tumblers behave like a coating-removal process: the laser removes or changes the top layer.
  • Bare stainless and aluminum need appropriate fiber/IR/fiber-like marking or a marking compound; ordinary blue diode/CO2 behavior differs by surface.

Leather, paper, cardboard and fabric

Low heatVentilation essentialTest for safety

  • Leather and paper darken quickly, so use lower power and higher speed than you might expect.
  • Fine photo dots can close up on soft fibers. Lower DPI or a lighter dithering mode may look better.
  • Confirm material composition. Artificial leather, vinyl and treated materials can be unsafe.

Make a photo test matrix

Do not test with a simple black square only. A photo needs highlights, skin/midtones and shadows. Use a 25–40 mm crop containing an eye, hair, skin and background. Duplicate it 6–12 times and test one variable at a time.

Power/speed matrix

Keep DPI and image fixed. Test several powers or speeds. Pick the version with the most detail, not necessarily the darkest mark.

DPI/interval matrix

Keep power and speed fixed. Test 250, 318, 333, 500 and 600 DPI if appropriate. Stop where detail stops improving.

Dither matrix

Keep laser settings fixed. Compare Floyd-Steinberg, Atkinson, Jarvis, Stucki and Ordered/Bayer on the same crop.

How to read test results

  • Too dark everywhere: reduce power, increase speed, lower DPI, brighten midtones, or use Atkinson.
  • Highlights missing: increase contrast in light areas, darken the image slightly, or choose a dither that places more dots in highlights.
  • Shadows are a black block: lift shadows with curves/gamma, lower power, or reduce dot density.
  • Faces look flat: add local contrast and sharpening before dithering; avoid over-smoothing skin.
  • Visible horizontal bands: check focus, belts, acceleration, line interval, dirty lens, excessive air assist or material unevenness.

Safety: do not skip this

Photo engraving often means long raster jobs. Long jobs create smoke, heat and fire risk. Use proper enclosure, exhaust, filtration where needed, eye protection suitable for the wavelength, and never leave the machine running unattended.

Never process unknown plastics. PVC/vinyl, halogen-containing materials, chromium-treated leather, PTFE/Teflon, PVB and some resins can create dangerous gases or corrosive residue. When in doubt, check the supplier’s SDS or do not laser it.
  • Keep the machine clean. Soot, paper scraps and wood dust increase fire risk.
  • Use proper ventilation, not just an open window.
  • Keep a suitable fire extinguisher nearby and know how to use it.
  • Use air assist appropriately; it helps with many materials but can change photo tone.
  • Do not engrave reflective bare metal with an unsuitable open diode setup.
  • Confirm coatings, paints and artificial leathers before lasering.

Advanced techniques

Material-specific image versions

Create different processed files for different materials. A wood version may need brighter shadows and lower DPI. A slate version may need inversion and stronger midtone contrast. A dark anodized aluminum version may need higher DPI and finer sharpening.

Selective editing

Do not treat the whole photo equally. Faces, eyes, hair, clothing and background need different contrast. Use masks or local adjustments when a single brightness/contrast slider cannot solve everything.

Defocus intentionally

A tiny intentional defocus can soften overly harsh dots on some materials, especially glass, acrylic or harsh diode burns. It can also reduce detail, so test carefully.

Multiple passes

For photos, one controlled pass is often cleaner than multiple hot passes. Multiple passes can help special materials, but they can also darken shadows, cause registration issues and increase heat damage.

Finishing

Cleaning and finish can change perceived contrast. Wood may need brushing and sealing. Slate may look different before and after cleaning. Acrylic may need washing, paint filling or backlighting depending on the project.

FAQ

What DPI is best for laser photo engraving?

There is no universal best DPI. Around 300–333 DPI is a practical starting point for many photo jobs, lower can be better on wood/stone with dot bleed, and 500–1000 DPI can be useful on acrylic or metal surfaces that hold fine dots. The best value is the one that matches your real laser spot and material response.

Should I use Floyd-Steinberg, Jarvis, Stucki or Atkinson?

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki. Try Atkinson when the engraving is too dark or you want a lighter vintage look. Try Jarvis when you want smoother skin tones and have enough material detail to support it. Always compare on the same small crop.

Should photos be inverted?

Invert when engraved areas become lighter than the surface, such as slate, glass, dark acrylic or dark anodized aluminum. Do not invert for light wood where burned areas become darker.

Why does my screen preview look better than the engraving?

A screen is backlit, high resolution and can show smooth grayscale. A laser makes physical marks on a material with grain, melting, soot, chipping or coating removal. That is why test matrices and material-specific preparation are essential.

Can I engrave any web photo?

Only if it has enough pixel resolution at the final size and good tonal separation. A 72 DPI web image is not automatically bad if it has enough pixels and is scaled down, but enlarging a tiny image rarely creates real detail.

PNG, BMP, JPG or SVG?

For photo engraving, PNG or BMP are common because they preserve raster pixels without JPEG compression artifacts. JPG is acceptable as a source if high quality, but avoid repeated JPG exports. SVG is mainly for vectors, not final dithered photo pixels.
