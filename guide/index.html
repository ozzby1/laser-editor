Quick start: the reliable five-step method

If you are new to laser photo engraving, do not begin by copying a random power and speed from the internet. Start with the material, create a small test crop, and tune only one variable at a time. This prevents the most common beginner problem: a preview that looks good on screen but turns into a muddy burn, pale frost or negative-looking engraving on the real surface.

Choose the material firstDecide whether the laser mark will become darker than the surface, lighter than the surface, frosted, bleached, melted or coating-removed. This determines inversion, contrast and the amount of energy you can use.
Use a suitable photoPick a sharp image with visible eyes, clear edges, separated subject and background, and usable detail in both shadows and highlights. A technically average photo can engrave better than a dramatic photo with blocked shadows.
Crop to the final physical sizeSet the real engraving size in millimeters before choosing DPI. A face that is too small cannot be saved by increasing DPI.
Prepare one clean image versionConvert to grayscale, tune levels/curves, adjust gamma, sharpen moderately after resizing, invert only when the material requires it, and avoid double-dithering.
Engrave a small photo test cropUse a 25–40 mm crop containing an eye, hair, skin, dark clothing and background. Test power/speed/interval on the same material before running the full engraving.
Best beginner rule: prepare the photo once, test a small crop, then change one thing at a time. If you change DPI, power, speed, contrast and dithering together, you will not know what fixed or ruined the result.

How laser photo engraving works

A laser engraving is not a printed photograph. A screen shows light; a laser creates physical marks. Depending on the surface, the mark can be a burn, frost, bleach, melt, chip, oxidation mark, coating removal or color change. That is why the same image can look excellent on slate and terrible on wood unless it is prepared differently.

Tone response

Some materials are almost binary: not marked, then suddenly dark or bright. These usually need dithering. Other materials can reproduce more intermediate tones and may work with grayscale power modulation.

Dot growth

Laser marks are rarely the exact size of the image pixel. Wood grain, leather fibers, glass chipping and heat spread make dots larger, so high DPI can overlap and reduce detail.

Viewing distance

A close-viewed pendant needs finer detail than a wall photo seen from two meters. Higher resolution is not always better; correct dot spacing for the viewing distance is better.

For most photo engraving, the goal is not maximum darkness. The goal is separation: highlights stay open, midtones carry the face, shadows keep texture, and the background does not compete with the subject.

Three ways a laser can represent a photograph

  • Dithered black/white raster: grayscale is converted into tiny black/white dots. This is predictable on materials with on/off behavior.
  • Grayscale raster: the laser varies power by pixel tone. This can be smooth, but only when the controller, laser source and material respond consistently.
  • Halftone/newsprint style: visible dot patterns are used intentionally for a printed-poster look. This is a style choice, not always the most natural portrait method.
Do not trust the monitor alone. A backlit preview hides smoke staining, grain, melting, dot overlap, focus errors and mechanical banding. The only reliable proof is a small real-material test.

Choosing a photo that will engrave well

The photo determines the ceiling of the result. Laser processing can improve contrast and tone separation, but it cannot create real facial detail that is not present in the original pixels.

LightingSoft directional light, visible cheek/forehead detail, separated shadows.Backlit face, black clothing merging with hair, blown-out forehead.Lift shadows with curves, reduce highlights, brighten eyes locally.

Portrait-specific rules

  • Eyes decide the emotional result. If the eyes disappear in the processed preview, the engraving will feel lifeless even if the rest is technically correct.
  • Skin needs midtones. Avoid crushing skin into pure white/black. Most portrait quality lives in gentle midtone changes.
  • Hair needs selective control. Dark hair often becomes a single burnt mass on wood unless shadows are lifted. Light hair on slate may need stronger contrast after inversion.
  • Do not waste pixels on empty background. A smaller, stronger crop usually engraves better than a full-body photo where the face is only a few millimeters wide.
Simple test before editing: view the photo small and in grayscale. If the subject is still readable, it is a good candidate. If the face blends into the background, fix that before dithering.

Image preparation workflow

This workflow works in browser tools such as PhotoEngraving.online, in LightBurn’s image tools, or in Photoshop, GIMP, Affinity Photo and similar editors. The order matters because every step changes what the next step sees.

Target tone ranges

For portraits, keep important facial detail away from pure black and pure white. On many materials, pure black areas become featureless burns or heavy frost, while pure white areas receive no useful mark. A strong laser-ready photo often looks slightly flatter and brighter on screen than a normal dramatic photo.

DPI, line interval and physical size

DPI is often misunderstood. On a laser it is not a magic quality slider. It is a way of describing how close the raster lines or pixels are. If your laser spot and material mark are larger than the pixel spacing, extra DPI makes the engraving darker, slower and muddier instead of sharper.

DPI myths

  • Higher DPI does not automatically mean more detail. If marks overlap, you lose highlights and midtones.
  • Image DPI metadata is not the same as useful laser DPI. What matters is pixel count at the final physical size.
  • Different lenses change the useful interval. A smaller focused spot can support finer intervals; a larger spot needs more spacing.
  • Material response matters as much as optics. Anodized aluminum can hold tiny marks better than wood grain or glass micro-fracture.

Dithering algorithms: which one to choose

Dithering converts grayscale into black and white dots. Different algorithms create different dot textures. There is no single winner; choose by image type, material, viewing distance and taste.

ModeLookBest useWatch out for
ThresholdPure black/white cutoff, no dot shading.Logos, icons, masks, already prepared 1-bit art.Bad for normal photos; loses midtones.
Ordered / BayerRegular grid pattern with controlled dot arrangement.Graphics, gradients, glass, acrylic, designs where predictable texture is acceptable.Pattern can be visible in portraits at close range.
Floyd-SteinbergNatural error-diffusion texture with crisp detail.Default all-rounder for many photos and materials.Can create diagonal artifacts in flat gradients.
AtkinsonLighter, softer, classic high-contrast dot look.Portraits that become too dark, vintage style, open highlights.Can lose detail in extreme shadows and highlights.
JarvisSmoother tonal transitions, rich detail, larger diffusion kernel.Portraits, smooth skin tones, larger photos where material can hold fine detail.Can look too soft or dense on wood/leather with dot bleed.
StuckiJarvis-like but often slightly sharper and faster.General photo engraving, good balance for wood, slate and acrylic tests.Midtones may need brightness/gamma tuning.
Sierra / BurkesError-diffusion variants between Floyd and Jarvis.Advanced comparison tests when your software offers them.Not always available in laser software.
Newsprint / HalftoneVisible print-style dot pattern.Decorative poster effect, retro graphics, intentional newspaper look.Not the most natural portrait look at close viewing distance.
GrayscaleNo black/white conversion; laser power varies by tone.Materials and machines with stable power modulation; relief/depth effects; some acrylic/metal workflows.Harder to tune; many materials are not linear.

Beginner choice

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki at a conservative DPI. If the engraving is too dark, test Atkinson or reduce density/power. If the photo needs smoother skin and the material holds detail, test Jarvis. For logos and clean graphic gradients, Ordered/Bayer can be better than error diffusion.

How to compare dithering fairly

  • Use the same crop, physical size, DPI, speed and power.
  • Judge from the real viewing distance, not only under a magnifier.
  • Check flat backgrounds for artifacts and faces for dirty-looking skin texture.
  • Do not compare one dither on wood and another on slate; material response changes everything.
Double dithering warning: if a PNG is already dithered into exact black/white pixels, another dithering pass can destroy the dot structure. Use pass-through or a mode that preserves the pixels.

Material-specific advice

Every material has its own “tone language”. Some get darker when engraved. Some get lighter. Some melt, frost, bleach or expose an underlayer. The same photo should often be inverted, sharpened or softened differently for each surface.

Light wood, plywood and bamboo

Usually not inverted. Use dithering, lower-to-medium DPI and moderate power. Light fine-grain woods are easiest. Open shadows before dithering so hair and clothes do not become one burnt mass. Sand consistently and clean soot before judging final contrast.

Slate and dark stone

Usually inverted. Engraved areas become lighter. Keep the process shallow; too much power chips the surface and destroys subtle tones. A photo crop matrix is more useful than copied settings.

Acrylic

Often inverted on dark/clear acrylic. Cast acrylic generally engraves cleaner than extruded acrylic for frosted photo effects. CO2 lasers excel on clear acrylic; blue diode lasers usually need opaque or dark acrylic.

Glass and mirrors

Often inverted. Glass frosts by micro-fracturing, not by burning black. Use masking or moist paper tests, reduce heat if chipping appears, and prefer lighter tone curves over dense black areas.

Leather

Usually not inverted on light leather. Use lower density and lower energy. Leather darkens quickly and can lose midtones. Confirm that the leather is safe to laser; avoid unknown treated or chrome-tanned materials.

Anodized or coated metal

Often inverted for bright marks on dark coatings. These surfaces can hold fine dots well, so 600–1000 DPI may be useful. Bare reflective metal needs the correct laser source and safety setup.

Painted tile / ceramic coating workflows

Photo detail depends on coating consistency and cleaning. Use a very small matrix first. Too much energy can over-fuse, over-burn or make cleanup difficult.

Paper, cardboard and fabric

Use low energy and fast motion. Photos can scorch or warp easily. These materials are better for stylized halftone or low-density dithering than dense photographic blacks.

Make a photo test matrix

Do not test with a simple black square only. A photo needs highlights, skin/midtones and shadows. Use a 25–40 mm crop containing an eye, hair, skin and background. Duplicate it 6–12 times and test one variable at a time.

Power/speed matrix

Keep DPI and image fixed. Test several power/speed combinations. Pick the lightest setting that still gives readable midtones.

Interval matrix

Keep power/speed fixed. Test line interval from coarse to fine. Stop when added density only darkens the image.

Dither matrix

Keep machine settings fixed. Compare Floyd, Stucki, Jarvis, Atkinson and Ordered where available.

What to record for repeatability

Save more than speed and power. Record machine type, laser source, lens/focus, air assist, material supplier, surface preparation, DPI/line interval, scan direction, image mode, inversion, cleanup method and finish. Without this, a good result is hard to reproduce.

Software workflow: PhotoEngraving.online, LightBurn, LaserGRBL and XCS

PhotoEngraving.online

Use the online editor when you want a fast browser-based workflow: upload a photo, select photo type, adjust brightness, contrast, gamma and sharpness, choose DPI/output size, apply dithering, inspect the result and export a laser-ready PNG. The editor is designed for private local browser processing, so the photo does not need to be uploaded to a server.

LightBurn

  • Use Image Mode for raster photos and choose the correct image mode: Threshold, Ordered, Atkinson, Dither, Stucki, Jarvis, Newsprint, Halftone or Grayscale.
  • Set line interval/DPI deliberately. Use Interval Test or Material Test for the actual laser/material/lens combination.
  • If the image is already dithered exactly as you want, use Pass-Through where appropriate to avoid re-processing.
  • Use Negative Image for slate, glass and other materials where engraved marks become lighter.
  • Use overscanning for raster jobs when your controller needs room to reach stable speed before firing.
  • For grayscale engraving, preview with power shading enabled where available so the preview represents variable power rather than a solid black scan.

LaserGRBL

  • Use “1bit BW dithering” for materials that behave mostly on/off.
  • Use line-to-line grayscale only when your laser supports power modulation and the material can reproduce intermediate shades.
  • Adjust brightness and contrast in the raster import workflow before engraving.
  • Confirm final size and offset in the target image dialog before sending the job.

xTool Creative Space / galvo dot workflows

  • Bitmap engraving may use dot duration, power, DPI, bitmap mode and laser type. These settings interact strongly.
  • For dual-source machines, choose the correct source: blue light for many organic materials, IR/fiber-type source for metals and selected plastics.
  • When using dot duration, excessive dwell time can whiten, over-mark or slow the job dramatically. Test small.

Pre-send checklist

  • Final physical size is correct in the laser software.
  • The image is not being resampled, auto-enhanced or dithered twice.
  • Inversion matches the material.
  • Line interval/DPI matches your test, not just the image metadata.
  • Air assist, focus, bed height and material orientation match the test piece.

Safety: do not skip this

Photo engraving often means long raster jobs. Long jobs create smoke, heat and fire risk. Use proper enclosure, exhaust, filtration where needed, eye protection suitable for the wavelength, and never leave the machine running unattended.

Never process unknown plastics. PVC/vinyl, halogen-containing materials, chromium-treated leather, PTFE/Teflon, PVB and some resins can create dangerous gases or corrosive residue. When in doubt, check the supplier’s SDS or do not laser it.
  • Keep the machine clean. Soot, paper scraps and wood dust increase fire risk.
  • Use proper ventilation, not just an open window.
  • Keep a suitable fire extinguisher nearby and know how to use it.
  • Use air assist appropriately; it helps with many materials but can change photo tone.
  • Do not engrave reflective bare metal with an unsuitable open diode setup.
  • Confirm coatings, paints and artificial leathers before lasering.

Advanced techniques

Material-specific image versions

Create different processed files for different materials. A wood version may need brighter shadows and lower DPI. A slate version may need inversion and stronger midtone contrast. A dark anodized aluminum version may need higher DPI and finer sharpening.

Selective editing

Do not treat the whole photo equally. Faces, eyes, hair, clothing and background need different contrast. Use masks or local adjustments when a single brightness/contrast slider cannot solve everything.

Controlled background simplification

For portraits, the background should support the subject, not compete with it. Blurring, lightening or removing the background often improves engraving more than increasing sharpness.

Intentional defocus

A tiny intentional defocus can soften overly harsh dots on some materials, especially glass, acrylic or harsh diode burns. It can also reduce detail, so test carefully and record the offset.

Scan direction

Wood grain, brushed metal and fabric weave can interact with scan direction. If banding or grain dominates the portrait, test rotating the image or changing scan angle where your machine supports it safely.

Multiple passes

For photos, one controlled pass is often cleaner than multiple hot passes. Multiple passes can help special materials, but they can also darken shadows, cause registration issues and increase heat damage.

Finishing

Cleaning and finish can change perceived contrast. Wood may need brushing and sealing. Slate may look different before and after cleaning. Acrylic may need washing, paint filling or backlighting depending on the project.

Final quality checklist before the full engraving

Use this checklist after the small test crop looks promising and before committing to a full-size engraving.

Image

Eyes readable, subject separated from background, shadows not crushed, highlights not empty, no JPEG noise or oversharpened halos.

Size

Final millimeter size confirmed, face large enough, pixel count matches chosen DPI, no accidental scaling in laser software.

Laser setup

Focus, lens, air assist, scan direction, overscan, bed position and material hold-down match the successful test.

Processing

Correct inversion, one dithering method only, pass-through used when needed, material-specific settings recorded.

Professional habit: keep a small engraved sample library with notes. Over time this becomes more valuable than any generic online settings table.

FAQ

What DPI is best for laser photo engraving?

There is no universal best DPI. Around 300–333 DPI is a practical starting point for many photo jobs, lower can be better on wood/stone with dot bleed, and 500–1000 DPI can be useful on acrylic or metal surfaces that hold fine dots. The best value is the one that matches your real laser spot and material response.

Should I use Floyd-Steinberg, Jarvis, Stucki or Atkinson?

Start with Floyd-Steinberg or Stucki. Try Atkinson when the engraving is too dark or you want a lighter vintage look. Try Jarvis when you want smoother skin tones and have enough material detail to support it. Always compare on the same small crop.

Should photos be inverted?

Invert when engraved areas become lighter than the surface, such as slate, glass, dark acrylic or dark anodized aluminum. Do not invert for light wood where burned areas become darker.

Is grayscale better than dithering?

Not always. Grayscale can be smooth when the laser and material respond linearly to power changes. Dithering is often more predictable on materials that behave mostly on/off, such as many woods, leathers and coated surfaces.

Why does my screen preview look better than the engraving?

A screen is backlit, high resolution and can show smooth grayscale. A laser makes physical marks on a material with grain, melting, soot, chipping or coating removal. That is why test matrices and material-specific preparation are essential.

Can I engrave any web photo?

Only if it has enough pixel resolution at the final size and good tonal separation. A 72 DPI web image is not automatically bad if it has enough pixels and is scaled down, but enlarging a tiny image rarely creates real detail.

Why should I avoid double dithering?

Dithering creates a specific pattern of black and white dots. If another program dithers that pattern again, the dot structure can become noisy, uneven or less detailed. A pre-dithered file should normally be preserved with pass-through or a true black/white workflow.

PNG, BMP, JPG or SVG?

For photo engraving, PNG or BMP are common because they preserve raster pixels without JPEG compression artifacts. JPG is acceptable as a source if high quality, but avoid repeated JPG exports. SVG is mainly for vectors, not final dithered photo pixels.
